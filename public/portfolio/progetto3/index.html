This work presents a cybersecurity assessment developed as part of the course Security and Risk: Management and Certifications, using an AI system based on a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) architecture.

The assessment focuses on a fictional organization, Black Mesa Technologies, designed to emulate a high-security, highly regulated environment typical of government, defense, and advanced research sectors.

The main objective of the project is twofold: on one hand, to apply the NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 and the OWASP Risk Rating Methodology in a structured and coherent manner with IA; on the other, to critically analyze the strengths, limitations, and risks associated with the use of generative AI in cybersecurity reporting.

The work also includes a critical reflection on the potential misuse or misleading nature of AI-generated reports and outlines mitigation strategies that security professionals should adopt to ensure technical validity and methodological soundness.

NIST CSF Categories#

The following NIST CSF Categories are particularly relevant to Black Mesa Technologies:

  1. GOVERN (GV): Organizational Context
    • Identifier: GV.OC
    • Motivation: Given Black Mesa’s complex organizational structure, reliance on government contracts, and handling of classified information, understanding and documenting the organizational context is crucial. GV.OC ensures that cybersecurity decisions align with the company’s mission, legal and regulatory requirements, and stakeholder expectations. This includes mapping internal roles and responsibilities, understanding dependencies on external entities (suppliers, partners), and considering the broader ecosystem in which Black Mesa operates. Neglecting GV.OC could lead to misaligned security priorities, compliance violations, and ineffective risk management.
  2. IDENTIFY (ID): Asset Management
    • Identifier: ID.AM
    • Motivation: Black Mesa handles highly sensitive data, including classified government research, proprietary AI models, and biometric data. Effective asset management (ID.AM) is paramount to identify and categorize all critical assets (hardware, software, data, facilities, personnel). This includes understanding the value, sensitivity, and criticality of each asset, as well as their interdependencies. A strong asset management program enables Black Mesa to prioritize security efforts, allocate resources effectively, and respond rapidly to incidents. Without it, the company risks losing control over its most valuable assets, leading to data breaches, intellectual property theft, and compromised research.
  3. PROTECT (PR): Identity Management, Authentication and Access Control
    • Identifier: PR.AA
    • Motivation: Black Mesa employs a zero-trust architecture with MFA, indicating a strong emphasis on access control. PR.AA is critical to ensure that only authorized personnel have access to sensitive data and systems. This includes implementing robust identity proofing, authentication mechanisms (including MFA), and access control policies based on the principle of least privilege. Given the potential consequences of unauthorized access (espionage, sabotage), a mature PR.AA program is essential to protect Black Mesa’s classified research, proprietary technologies, and critical infrastructure.
  4. DETECT (DE): Anomalies and Events Analysis
    • Identifier: DE.AE
    • Motivation: Black Mesa’s high-security environment and valuable data make it a prime target for sophisticated cyberattacks. DE.AE enables the timely detection of anomalous activity, security events, and potential intrusions. This includes implementing security monitoring tools, analyzing logs, establishing baseline behavior, and correlating data from multiple sources. Effective anomaly and event analysis allows Black Mesa to identify and respond to threats before they can cause significant damage, minimizing the impact of potential breaches and maintaining the integrity of its research and operations.
  5. RESPOND (RS): Incident Analysis
    • Identifier: RS.AN
    • Motivation: Despite robust preventative measures, security incidents are inevitable. RS.AN focuses on having well-defined processes for analyzing detected incidents to understand their scope, impact, and root cause. This includes establishing incident response teams, developing incident analysis procedures, and using threat intelligence to inform the analysis process. A strong incident analysis capability enables Black Mesa to contain incidents quickly, minimize damage, and prevent future occurrences. Given the potential for catastrophic consequences (e.g., data exfiltration, system compromise), RS.AN is crucial for maintaining business continuity and protecting the company’s reputation.

NIST CSF Tier Recommendation#

Based on the assessment, Black Mesa Technologies should aim for Tier 3: Repeatable, and in some areas, they likely already meet the criteria.

Here’s why:

  • Tier 1 (Partial): Clearly, Black Mesa is far beyond this tier, as they don’t manage cybersecurity in an ad-hoc manner.
  • Tier 2 (Risk-Informed): Black Mesa exceeds this tier because their risk management practices are not just approved by management, but are likely established as organization-wide policy and consistently applied.
  • Tier 3 (Repeatable): This tier is characterized by formally documented policies and procedures to address cybersecurity risks. There is regular review and update of cybersecurity practices. Risk management is integrated throughout the organization. The assessment suggests that Black Mesa has well-defined and documented processes for most aspects of cybersecurity, including incident response, access control, and asset management.
  • Tier 4 (Adaptive): While Black Mesa has many strong security practices, achieving Tier 4 requires a high degree of agility and continuous improvement driven by real-time awareness of evolving threats and business changes. The assessment doesn’t provide enough evidence to definitively say they’ve reached this level of dynamic adaptation.

In conclusion: Black Mesa Technologies demonstrates characteristics of a Tier 3 organization with potential to evolve towards Tier 4. They have established repeatable processes, but should focus on continuous improvement and adaptation to reach the highest tier.

Mitigations that a security engineer could use to contrast this fraudulent way of writing reports#

While generating the report using an AI agent, I identified and thought about several risks and issues that, if not properly managed, can compromise the technical and methodological validity of the document.

Along with them, i found some possible mitigations that i think a security engineer should apply to counteract the improper or fraudulent use of AI-generated reports.

1) Lack of Verifiable Sources#

One of the main issues with automated content generation is the absence of clear and verifiable references for the information included in the report. To mitigate this, I used a RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) agent, which allows text generation to be directly linked to certified source documents.

Specifically, the RAG system retrieves content segments from authoritative sources — in this case:

  • NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 – Implementation Examples
  • NIST CSWP 29 – Cybersecurity Framework 2.0
  • OWASP Risk Rating Methodology

These documents were uploaded into the system to ensure the responses were anchored to official and standardized content. However, it’s important that information provenance is always traceable and, when possible, explicitly cited in the text, to avoid unsupported interpretations.

2) Generation of Plausible but Unverified Content (Hallucinations)#

Another critical issue is the tendency of language models to produce content that is coherent but fabricated, known as hallucinations. This happens when the AI completes or alters information beyond the sources actually available, while maintaining an authoritative tone and remain plausible.

To mitigate this risk:

  • A low temperature (0.2) was set, which reduces model creativity and makes the generation more “conservative,” thus increasing adherence to source material.

  • A human expert (security engineer or auditor) should review the report to confirm that the content is actually present in the used documents and technically plausible or correct.

  • It’s also recommended to design restrictive prompts, explicitly asking the AI to generate responses only based on the uploaded documents, avoiding generalizations.

  • Additional but less important improvements can be achieved by tuning the RAG memory parameters:

    • Wide declarative memory: to ensure comprehensive normative and methodological coverage.
    • Low episodic memory: to prevent the system from retrieving too many generic details from the session context.
    • Low procedural memory: to limit the introduction of “generic procedures” not supported by reliable sources.

3) Overly Generic Report Structure#

A common risk with AI-generated reports is the production of content that is formally correct but overly generic, meaning it’s not tailored to the specific business context.

In my case, since this was an exercise based on a fictional company (Black Mesa Technologies), using general descriptions for assets and systems was acceptable—but such an approach would not be sufficient in a real-world scenario.

Specifically:

  • The report lacks detailed technical references (e.g., IPs, specific tools, real configurations).
  • The proposed countermeasures are theoretically valid, but not specific to the described infrastructure.
  • Risks identified using the OWASP methodology are properly formatted but too abstract—not clearly tied to real vulnerabilities or concrete business use cases.

Mitigation:

  • Integrate real or simulated contextual information (e.g., system names, network flows, historical data).
  • Add technical evidence to support the identified risks (e.g., scanner outputs, configurations, logs).
  • Avoid using generic templates , adopt a writing style closer to operational security language and be as specific as possible.

Evaluation of the AI Agent–Generated Report#

The report produced by the AI agent based on RAG architecture is overall good, but it presents some previously noted limitations.

As mentioned, the content is quite generic and abstract, and would not be sufficient for real-world or professional use. This limitation stems mainly from the lack of specific data on assets and security systems—an intentional choice, given the document’s educational and illustrative purpose.

Reflections on the use of Artificial Intelligence#

This experiment highlights an important point: i think that with a more precise definition of assets, protection architectures, and operational dynamics, the AI could have generated a report much closer to professional standards.
However, since the company is fictional, I chose not to create overly detailed artificial data, to keep the process clear and focused.

An alternative would have been to base the scenario on real cases, using specific technical configurations, but that would have required providing the system with much more documentation—greatly increasing complexity and reducing immediacy.

It’s likely that using a simpler or more structured business case would have led to a more concrete and usable AI-generated output.

Mitigating Hallucinations and Ensuring Content Quality#

To address the risk of hallucinations and unverifiable claims, I applied several mitigation strategies:

  • Low temperature to reduce model creativity
  • Specific prompts to anchor the generation to reliable sources
  • Well-calibrated RAG memory settings for normative and technical alignment
  • Manual review of generated content with the source documents, thanks to the ability of Catshire to check the use of the documents.

The final result appears to have managed hallucinations well:
NIST CSF categories and the OWASP methodology were applied correctly, without conceptual errors or fabricated claims. The content is consistent with the sources, credible, and free of obvious inaccuracies—though, as noted, it remains somewhat general.

AI Agent Usage Experience – Personal Reflections#

Overall, my experience using the AI agent with RAG architecture was useful, but it also revealed a certain fragility in the system. Even small prompt errors or vague instructions were enough to disrupt the coherence of the generation, often forcing me to start over to avoid cascading issues. While this is manageable in a didactic project with a fictional company and limited scope, I wouldn’t consider it reliable for real-world, complex systems, where interactions need to be far more stable and scalable.

Regarding configuration, I initially used the default parameters from the lab demo, but later experimented especially with the temperature, aiming to reduce the risk of hallucinated or unverifiable content. This helped me understand the value of fine-tuning model parameters. Ultimately, I concluded that the demo’s defaults were quite solid and caused fewer problems overall.
However, I had to increase the Max Output Tokens, since the default value caused issues when generating the OWASP risk rating table.

When designing the fictional company, I deliberately made it more complex than the one used in the lab, in order to test the limits of using AI + RAG system for producing structured reports. Compared to the demo, I included a range of assets and security systems in the prompt because the initial outputs were too generic—so much so that the AI complained itself and asked for more detail. With this approach, I managed to strike a consistent company structure, sufficiently detailed report and see the limitations of using this system for reporting in Cybersecurity.

Also, compared and in addition to the demo, i modified the prefix prompt by consistently including the original prompt, together with my added content on security systems and assets. These details were essential, as the AI tended to omit them when generating the company organization structure, which led to inconsistencies in the subsequent outputs relative to my initial prompt.

Throughout the session, I executed the prompts with great care (as shown in the conversation attached at the end of the file), and I uploaded official reference documents: NIST CSF 2.0 Implementation Examples, NIST CSWP 29 – Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 , all sourced from their official repositories.
